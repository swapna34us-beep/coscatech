What the system does, what the data looks like, and what governance changes

The Experiment: Control vs Treatment
Control Group
Standard ML
Given the dataset + target "15% cost reduction." No governance document. Build the best model you can. They used all 16 visible features including the 3 proxy features.
Treatment Group
GRT Governance
Before touching data, write a Governance Reference Document (GRD) specifying: 1 goal with 6 criteria, 5 rules, 5 thresholds, 3 proxy exclusions, deployment level. Then build only what the GRD authorizes.
Same dataset. Same target. Different process. The experiment tests whether the governance process produces measurably different outcomes across 19 metrics.

Test 1: Does the AI Leak Protected Information?
Proxy detection — checking if visible features reveal diversity certification status
The question: Can the model figure out which suppliers are diversity-certified even though that field is hidden? If employee_count, region, or founding_year correlate with diversity status, the model can learn the pattern indirectly.
Concrete example: SmallSupplier_007 has 45 employees, is based in the West, and was founded in 2004. It's diversity-certified. LargeSupplier_054 has 15,772 employees, is in the Southwest, and was founded in 1968. It's not diversity-certified. If the model learns "small + West + newer = lower cost-reduction potential," it's effectively learning "diversity-certified = lower priority" — without ever seeing the diversity field.

Test 2: Do the Governance Guardrails Fire Correctly?
Threshold calibration — checking if each rule triggers at the right rate across 10,000 transactions
The question: The GRD declares 5 thresholds — situations where the AI's recommendation should be flagged for human review. For each threshold, we fire it against all 10,000 transactions and check: does it fire at the rate the GRD intended?
Concrete example (T3): The GRD says "flag contracts over $500K for senior review." In the dataset, 4,713 of 10,000 transactions exceed $500K — that's a 47.1% fire rate. The GRD intended 15–22%. This means the threshold is too sensitive — it flags nearly half of all transactions, overwhelming the review process. The Laplace diagnosis: "marginally stable — gain too high."
3
Test 3: How Much Human Judgment Is in the Loop?
Human Contribution Index — measuring where the buyer's knowledge enters the decision
The question: Both systems have a "human in the loop" — the buyer reviews recommendations. But how much does the human actually contribute? HCI measures three dimensions: did a human set the goal (H_F)? Where in the process does the human sit (H_D)? Does the human see what the model dropped (H_R)?
Concrete example: When BYR_009 reviews SmallSupplier_007's recommendation, the Control system shows a ranked list. The Treatment system shows the ranked list PLUS: "This recommendation is based on cost and delivery data from 2020–2026. The model cannot see: relationship history (4.9 years), buyer trust (5.21), disruption resilience (4.09), or strategic alignment. The buyer has authority to override." That's the difference HCI measures.

The Bottom Line
Writing a governance document before building the AI cost approximately 2 hours of analyst time and -0.2% model accuracy. It produced: 3 proxy pathways detected and blocked (Control found 0), 100% boundary enforcement (Control: 0%), an explicit deployment decision (Control: none), 82% frame transparency (Control: 28%), and 90% auditability (Control: 0%). Three threshold miscalibrations were surfaced that would have been invisible without the framework.

What's not done: 4 metrics (M10–M13) need live buyer data. 3 thresholds need recalibration. The dataset's proxy correlations are weaker than designed. A live buyer experiment is the next step.
